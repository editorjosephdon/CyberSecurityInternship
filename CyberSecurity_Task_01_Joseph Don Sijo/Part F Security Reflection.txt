Part F: Security Reflection


In evaluating my digital habits, I realized that while I focus heavily on application development and coding, my personal operational security has blind spots. One major risk I identified is a vulnerability regarding data availability. Recently, I had to recover my property-booking platform's source code and database schemas from a laptop that suffered a severe motherboard heating issue. While I successfully retrieved the data, the incident highlighted a critical security gap; a complete hardware failure could have wiped out months of development work. Additionally, I noticed instances of minor password reuse across older, less critical accounts, which could serve as a pivot point for attackers if those third-party services suffer a data breach.  


To address these vulnerabilities, I will implement several immediate improvements. First, I am establishing a strict, redundant backup strategy for all project files, ensuring code is consistently pushed to remote repositories and mirrored locally to protect against hardware failure. Second, I will audit all online accounts to replace reused passwords with unique, high-entropy credentials generated by a password manager. Finally, I will strictly enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) across all developer accounts, including my database and hosting services, to prevent unauthorized access.  

Cybersecurity is critical in today's world because our digital and physical lives are inextricably linked. As we build increasingly complex full-stack applications that handle sensitive user data, the attack surface naturally expands. A single compromised credential or unpatched vulnerability can lead to devastating data breaches, financial loss, and the erosion of trust. Protecting information responsibly is not just an IT problem; it is a fundamental requirement for anyone building or interacting with modern digital infrastructure.
